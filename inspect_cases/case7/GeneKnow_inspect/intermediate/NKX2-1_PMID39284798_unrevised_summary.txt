NKX2-1 controls foregut endoderm transcriptional programs and is required for branching morphogenesis, and during early development it directs distinct AT2 versus AT1 fate choices from SOX9 progenitors. NKX2-1 is required in AT2 cells to induce diverse transcriptional targets, and NKX2-1 directly regulates genes encoding surfactant proteins A–D and genes controlling AT2 lipid homeostasis including Abca3, Scd1, and Sreb7. NKX2-1 regulatory actions induce surfactant lipids, surfactant proteins, and innate host defense protein synthesis prior to birth, and incomplete AT2 differentiation in preterm infants is associated with surfactant deficiency causing respiratory distress syndrome. In perinatal AT2 cell differentiation before birth, NKX2-1 activity is modulated by phosphorylation and by changes in chromatin accessibility. NKX2-1 transcriptional targets increase dramatically before birth despite no concomitant increase in NKX2-1 expression. NKX2-1-dependent regulation controls perinatal AT2 cell differentiation and surfactant homeostasis, and this regulation is mediated by epigenomic modulation via PRDM3 and PRDM16, which regulate chromatin accessibility at NKX2-1 transcriptional targets. Combined deletion of PRDM3 and PRDM16 in early lung endoderm causes perinatal lethality due to loss of AT2 cells and accumulation of partially differentiated AT1 cells, and lineage-specific deletion of PRDM3/16 in AT2 cells leads to lineage infidelity with PRDM3/16-null cells acquiring partial AT1 fate.
