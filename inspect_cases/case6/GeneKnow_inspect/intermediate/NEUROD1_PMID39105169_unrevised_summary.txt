NEUROD1 is a transcription factor in the nervous system that was initially characterized for converting nonneural ectodermal cells into neurons in Xenopus embryos. Studies in Caenorhabditis elegans, Drosophila melanogaster, and mice indicate NEUROD1 and its homologs act as critical regulators of neuronal progenitor cell differentiation and neuronal specification. In pluripotent mouse ES cells ectopically induced NEUROD1 overrides the pluripotent state and promotes neurogenesis. NEUROD1 directly binds promoters and enhancers of neuronal developmental genes. Chromatin immunoprecipitation in mouse E13.5 cortex confirmed binding to regulatory elements of Pou3f2, Insm1, Nhlh1, Atoh1, Neurod4, and Nhlh2. In the mouse inner ear NEUROD1 is essential for initiation of neurogenesis, delamination of neuroblasts, and survival of early neurons. Neurod1 deletion causes substantial defects in sensory neurons of vestibular and spiral ganglia. In the olfactory epithelium NEUROD1 elimination does not affect initial sensory neuron differentiation but compromises production and survival of mature neurons. In the mouse retina NEUROD1 deficiency leads to progressive degeneration of rod and cone photoreceptors and delays amacrine cell differentiation. Limited effects in some regions may reflect redundancy with other bHLH factors such as NEUROD2, NEUROD4, NSCL1/NHLH1, and NEUROG2.
