Systematic: FOXP3 is the lineage‑determining transcription factor that forms a large molecular complex with multiple transcription factors and enzymatic subunits to dynamically regulate the development and function of regulatory T cells. In healthy people FOXP3+ regulatory T cells maintain immune homeostasis. In cancer tumour‑infiltrating FOXP3+ regulatory T cells have strong immunosuppressive function and may promote immune escape and tumour development. Tumour‑derived factors modulate the induction, migration, and immunosuppressive function of FOXP3+ regulatory T cells. In cancer VEGF promotes migration of tumour‑infiltrating FOXP3+ regulatory T cells and enhances their immunosuppressive function. Clinical studies report that antiangiogenic therapy blocking VEGFR is associated with reduced tumour‑infiltrating FOXP3+ regulatory T cells and that the mechanisms linking VEGFR blockade to this reduction remain unclear. In colorectal cancer a tissue‑resident FOXP3(lo) subset lacking the naïve marker CD45RA displays FOXP3 instability and enhanced secretion of inflammatory cytokines such as IFN‑γ. Fusobacterium nucleatum mediates tumour production of IL‑12, TGF‑β, and TNF‑α. These cytokines affect tumour‑infiltrating regulatory T cell heterogeneity and facilitate expansion of pro‑inflammatory FOXP3(lo) non‑Treg cells. The pro‑inflammatory FOXP3(lo) non‑Treg cells enhance anti‑tumour immunity, inhibit tumour formation, and are predicted to confer better survival in colorectal cancer patients.
