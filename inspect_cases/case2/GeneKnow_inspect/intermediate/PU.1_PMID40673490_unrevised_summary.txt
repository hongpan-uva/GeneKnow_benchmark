PU.1 (SPI1) governs development of the macrophage lineage in mice and acts as a master regulator for macrophage and microglia development in fish and mammals. PU.1 disruption causes embryonic lethality and complete absence of macrophages including microglia in mice. In pu.1-null zebrafish mutants macrophage and microglia development is completely blocked at early development. PU.1 dosage is highly sensitive for the survival and maintenance of microglia in teleosts and mammals. As a master regulator PU.1 controls genes required for macrophage and microglia development. PU.1 loss might cause downregulation of Csf1r and loss of anti-apoptotic genes such as BCL2A1. PU.1 loss might also lead to absence of other genes that maintain cell integrity. Conditional ablation of PU.1 in mice or of Pu.1/Spi‑b in zebrafish leads to rapid depletion of microglia. Conditional inactivation by tamoxifen in CK‑p25 mice produced a reduction in microglia at young ages. High PU.1 expression in terminally differentiated microglia implies regulation of microglial behaviors and functions in the CNS. ChIP‑Seq in BV2 microglial cells revealed thousands of PU.1 targets. SiRNA silencing of PU.1 in mixed human glial culture reduced microglial viability and phagocytosis of Aβ42. An ER‑fusion inactivation study found no effect on alveolar macrophage viability. Conditional or pharmacological PU.1 inactivation in an AD mouse model ameliorated neuroinflammation. The same inactivation prevented microgliosis and improved cognition. Whether PU.1 directly regulates microglial survival in vivo remains unresolved.
