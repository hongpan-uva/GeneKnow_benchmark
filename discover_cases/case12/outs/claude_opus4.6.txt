PD-1 (programmed cell death protein 1) is an immune checkpoint receptor expressed on activated T cells that, upon engagement with its ligands PD-L1 and PD-L2, delivers inhibitory signals that suppress T-cell proliferation, cytokine production, and cytolytic function, thereby attenuating antitumor immunity (Keir et al., 2008). Melanoma cells frequently upregulate PD-L1 expression, which enables them to exploit this pathway to evade immune destruction (Taube et al., 2012). Blockade of the PD-1/PD-L1 axis with monoclonal antibodies such as nivolumab and pembrolizumab has demonstrated significant improvements in overall survival and objective response rates in patients with advanced melanoma compared to chemotherapy and to the anti-CTLA-4 antibody ipilimumab (Robert et al., 2015; Larkin et al., 2015). Combination therapy using nivolumab plus ipilimumab has yielded higher response rates and longer progression-free survival than either agent alone in untreated advanced melanoma, though with increased immune-related adverse events (Larkin et al., 2015). Long-term follow-up data have shown durable responses in a substantial proportion of patients, with five-year overall survival rates exceeding 40% for anti-PD-1 monotherapy and exceeding 50% for the combination regimen (Larkin et al., 2019).
