PD-1 functions as an inhibitory immune checkpoint receptor on T cells [1-3]. PD-1 serves as a therapeutic target in melanoma [2,4,5]. Specifically, PD-1 blockade provides clinical benefit in melanoma but many advanced tumors exhibit innate resistance [4]. Neoepitopes from somatic non-synonymous mutations are critical for benefit [4]. The IPRES transcriptional signature denotes up-expression of mesenchymal/ECM/angiogenesis genes and mediates innate anti-PD-1 resistance [4]. Melanoma extracellular-vesicle PD-L1 binds PD-1 [1]. Exosomal PD-L1 binds PD-1 on antigen-experienced CD8 T cells and inhibits antigen-experienced CD8 T cells [1]. Anti-PD-L1 or anti-PD-1 antibodies block the inhibition of antigen-experienced CD8 T cells [1]. Early on-treatment increases in circulating exosomal PD-L1 stratify responders from non-responders [1]. The PD-1–PD-L1 pathway is identified as important for treating some cancers [2]. An anti–PD-1 antibody showed PD-1–receptor occupancy on circulating CD3+ T cells and produced clinical activity in melanoma, including one complete response [2]. In human melanoma and murine tumor models, anti-PD-1 predominantly induces expansion of specific tumor-infiltrating exhausted-like CD8 T cell subsets [3]. The findings indicate checkpoint blockade targets only particular tumor-infiltrating T cells and anti-PD-1 responses are driven by mechanisms distinct from anti-CTLA-4 [3]. Anti-PD-1 therapy produces objective responses in melanoma [5]. Defects in interferon-receptor signaling and antigen presentation were associated with acquired resistance to PD-1 blockade [5]. Some responders later experienced progression or delayed relapse despite ongoing therapy [5].
