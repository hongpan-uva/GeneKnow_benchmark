Programmed cell death protein 1 (PD-1) is an inhibitory checkpoint receptor expressed on tumor-infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs), including $\alpha\beta$ and $\gamma\delta$ T cells, in the melanoma microenvironment (Herold et al., 2026; Pardoll, 2012). Melanoma cells frequently express the ligand programmed death-ligand 1 (PD-L1), which binds PD-1 to recruit Src homology region 2 domain-containing phosphatase (SHP-2). This interaction dephosphorylates the T-cell receptor (TCR) complex, thereby inducing T-cell exhaustion and suppressing cytokine production (Jiang et al., 2013; Tong et al., 2025). This axis facilitates adaptive immune resistance, as interferon-gamma ($IFN-\gamma$) produced by immune cells paradoxically upregulates PD-L1 on tumor cells to evade destruction (Tumeh et al., 2014). Clinical blockade of PD-1 with monoclonal antibodies such as pembrolizumab or nivolumab restores anti-tumor activity; final 10-year outcomes from the CheckMate 067 trial show a median overall survival (OS) of 71.9 months for nivolumab plus ipilimumab versus 19.9 months for ipilimumab alone (Robert et al., 2015; Wolchok et al., 2025). Furthermore, dual inhibition of PD-1 and LAG-3 significantly improves progression-free survival in treatment-naive metastatic melanoma (Tawbi et al., 2022). Recently, high baseline peripheral V$\delta$1 T-cell frequencies were identified as a specific predictor of reduced OS under anti-PD-1 therapy (Herold et al., 2026).
