Article Summary

The PD-1–PD-L1 pathway is identified as important for treating some cancers, and signals of clinical activity have been observed in patients with melanoma who were treated with an anti–PD-1 antibody. The anti–PD-1 antibody showed PD-1–receptor occupancy on circulating CD3+ T cells (median 64–70% by dose level after one cycle in 65 patients with melanoma treated at 0.1–10.0 mg/kg every 2 weeks). These signals of clinical activity and the biomarker-based selection approach need prospective assessment. Phase 2 trials with immunologic and molecular-marker correlates are under way. Phase 3 studies of anti–PD-1 antibody for melanoma are being planned. One patient with melanoma who received 3.0 mg/kg of anti–PD-1 antibody had a complete response. Median time to peak concentration was 1 to 4 hours after infusion start, and pharmacokinetics were linear with dose-proportional increases across the 0.1–10.0 mg/kg range. Median PD-1–receptor occupancy on circulating CD3+ T cells was 64–70% by dose level after one cycle (0.1–10.0 mg/kg every 2 weeks) in 65 patients with melanoma.

This study and a companion study of anti–PD-L1 antibody, now reported in the Journal , 26 describe clinical activity with these agents that validates the importance of the PD-1–PD-L1 pathway for the treatment of some cancers. The signals of clinical activity in patients with non–small-cell lung cancer, melanoma, and renal-cell cancer treated with anti–PD-1 antibody and the possibility of basing patient selection for such treatment on PD-L1 expression in tumors need to be prospectively assessed. Phase 2 trials involving immunologic and molecular-marker correlates (ClinicalTrials.gov numbers, NCT01354431 and NCT01358721 ) are under way, and phase 3 studies of anti–PD-1 antibody for the treatment of non–small-cell lung cancer, melanoma, and renal-cell cancer are being planned. An assessment of such treatment for other tumor types is also of interest.
