NOTCH1

NOTCH1 functions as an oncogenic transcriptional regulator that drives T-cell leukemogenesis [1-3]. NOTCH1 is a recurrent target of mutation across leukemias including T-ALL and CLL [1,4,5]. In chronic lymphocytic leukemia, NOTCH1 mutations promote cell proliferation [5]. NOTCH1 mutations are associated with trisomy 12, unmutated IGHV, inferior responses to chemoimmunotherapy, venetoclax, and ibrutinib, and with higher risk of transformation [5]. In T-ALL, NOTCH1 acts as an oncogene that upregulates Hes1 [1]. Upregulated Hes1 sustains AKT and NF-κB signaling and inhibits apoptosis [1]. In B-CLL, NOTCH1 PEST-domain mutations associate with poor prognosis [1]. ICN1 expression drives a NOTCH1 transcriptional program and γ-secretase inhibitor sensitivity [1]. NOTCH1 reads thymic signals to commit lymphoid progenitors to T-cell fate [2]. Activating NOTCH1 mutations cause constitutive activation requiring gamma-secretase cleavage [2]. Constitutively active NOTCH1 activates MYC [2]. NOTCH1 inhibition increases leukemia reliance on glutaminolysis and synergizes with glutaminase inhibition [2]. NOTCH1 is frequently activated in T-ALL [3]. NOTCH1 dimerization–dependent signaling drives a gene signature including HES4 [3]. HES4 enforces ∆133p53 expression and induces BCL2L1 [3]. NOTCH1 dimerization is crucial for T-ALL leukemogenesis and a dimerization-defective NOTCH1-ΔE-R1984A failed to generate leukemia [3]. NOTCH1 is a known target of mutation in T-ALL and is altered at greater frequency than previously recognized [4].
