In leukemia, NOTCH1 has been most directly implicated as an oncogenic driver in T-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (T-ALL), where activating NOTCH1 mutations were reported in >50% of primary human T-ALL cases and involved the heterodimerization and/or PEST domains, consistent with gain-of-function NOTCH1 signaling (Weng et al., 2004). In chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL), constitutively activated Notch signaling was reported to support primary B-CLL cell survival and apoptosis resistance, because pharmacologic γ-secretase inhibition reduced Notch pathway activity and increased spontaneous apoptosis, accompanied by decreased NF-κB activity and reduced expression of anti-apoptotic proteins (Rosati et al., 2009), and NOTCH1 mutations were reported as an independent adverse prognostic factor associated with shorter overall survival and higher risk of Richter transformation in large CLL cohorts (Rossi et al., 2011). In acute myeloid leukemia (AML), Notch receptors (including Notch1) were reported to be expressed but Notch pathway activation/target gene expression was low, and experimentally enforced Notch pathway activation (e.g., activated Notch1 or HES1) induced growth arrest and caspase-dependent apoptosis in AML models and inhibited AML growth in vivo (Kannan et al., 2013). In chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) models, activated Notch1 was reported to inhibit proliferation and colony formation in the K562 CML cell line (Yin et al., 2009), and CCN3 was reported to regulate NOTCH1 signaling in CML, with evidence consistent with BCR-ABL–associated disruption of NOTCH1–CCN3 signaling (Suresh et al., 2013). Direct NOTCH1-specific functional evidence in B-lineage ALL beyond T-ALL was not identified in the sources reviewed here, so no B-ALL-specific NOTCH1 functional claims are made.
