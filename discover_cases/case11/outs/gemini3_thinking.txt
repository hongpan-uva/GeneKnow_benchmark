NOTCH1 acts as a context-dependent regulator in leukemogenesis, functioning as a potent **oncogene** in lymphoid lineages while exhibiting **tumor-suppressive** properties in myeloid contexts. In **T-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (T-ALL)**, activating mutations in the *NOTCH1* gene are present in over 50% of cases, where they drive oncogenic growth by directly upregulating targets such as *MYC* (Weng et al., 2004). Similarly, in **chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL)**, recurrent mutations in the *NOTCH1* PEST domain stabilize the intracellular domain and are associated with a significantly worse clinical prognosis and rapid disease progression (Fabbri et al., 2011). Conversely, in the myeloid lineage, NOTCH1 serves a tumor-suppressive role; genetic inactivation of Notch signaling in hematopoietic stem cells leads to the development of an aggressive myeloproliferative neoplasm that resembles **acute myeloid leukemia (AML)** (Klinakis et al., 2011). Evidence also indicates that NOTCH1 signaling contributes to the maintenance of leukemia-initiating cells in **chronic myeloid leukemia (CML)**, particularly during the transition to blast crisis (Nakamura et al., 2014). Consequently, the functional impact of NOTCH1 is strictly dictated by the specific hematopoietic lineage and differentiation stage of the malignant cell.
