NOTCH1 is a transmembrane receptor that functions as a ligand-activated transcription factor and plays a critical role in T-cell development and leukemogenesis. Activating mutations in *NOTCH1* were identified in over 50% of human T-cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (T-ALL) cases, most commonly involving the heterodimerization (HD) domain and the C-terminal PEST domain, leading to ligand-independent signaling or increased stability of the intracellular NOTCH1 domain (Weng et al., 2004). The original connection between NOTCH1 and leukemia was established through characterization of the t(7;9)(q34;q34.3) chromosomal translocation, which juxtaposes a truncated *NOTCH1* allele (termed *TAN1*) with the TCRβ locus in a subset of T-ALL patients (Ellisen et al., 1991). Oncogenic NOTCH1 signaling in T-ALL activates downstream targets including *MYC* and the PI3K-AKT pathway, promoting cell growth and survival (Palomero et al., 2006). Gamma-secretase inhibitors (GSIs), which block proteolytic cleavage required for NOTCH1 activation, have demonstrated anti-leukemic effects in T-ALL cell lines and preclinical models, though gastrointestinal toxicity has limited clinical application (Real et al., 2009). Loss-of-function mutations in *FBXW7*, which normally targets NOTCH1 for proteasomal degradation, represent an additional mechanism of aberrant NOTCH1 activation in T-ALL (Thompson et al., 2007).
