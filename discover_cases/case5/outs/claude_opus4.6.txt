MYOD1 (Myogenic Differentiation 1) is a basic helix-loop-helix (bHLH) transcription factor that serves as a master regulator of skeletal muscle cell determination and differentiation. MYOD1 was the first transcription factor shown to reprogram non-muscle cells — including fibroblasts, nerve, fat, and liver cells — into skeletal muscle cells through forced expression (Tapscott, 2005). In adult muscle, MYOD1 is absent from quiescent muscle stem cells (satellite cells) but is rapidly upregulated upon activation via translational control mechanisms, initiating skeletal muscle gene expression during the early phase of regeneration (Tsuji et al., 2025). MYOD1 orchestrates critical cell fate transitions including quiescence-to-activation, proliferation, differentiation initiation, and self-renewal of muscle stem cells (Tsuji et al., 2025). Activated satellite cells expressing MYOD1 downregulate Pax7, and a subset subsequently express Myogenin, leading to terminal differentiation into myotubes and myofiber maturation (Tsuji et al., 2025). Mechanistically, MYOD1 promotes cell cycle exit in differentiating muscle cells by enhancing transcription of p21 and Myogenin (Zammit, 2017). Beyond transcriptional activation, MYOD1 also functions as a three-dimensional genome organizer, establishing muscle cell–specific chromatin architecture including compartment switching and chromatin loop formation (Wang et al., 2022). Single-knockout studies demonstrate that MYOD1 uniquely regulates adult muscle regeneration, influencing myogenic differentiation timing, self-renewal capacity, and apoptosis resistance (Tsuji et al., 2025).
