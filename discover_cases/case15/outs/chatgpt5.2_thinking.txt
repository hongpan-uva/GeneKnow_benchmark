FOXA1 has been experimentally shown to shape androgen receptor (AR) chromatin binding and AR-regulated transcriptional output in prostate cancer models: in LNCaP-derived cells, FOXA1 depletion caused extensive redistribution of AR chromatin-binding sites accompanied by changes in androgen-responsive gene expression programs (Sahu et al., 2011), and mechanistic analyses in prostate cancer cells showed FOXA1 can open local chromatin and facilitate AR recruitment at low-affinity sites while FOXA1 downregulation enabled widespread AR binding and AR target gene expression even under androgen-deprived conditions (Jin et al., 2014). Beyond AR-dependent transcriptional effects, FOXA1 was reported to have an AR-independent metastasis-suppressive function: in prostate cancer cell models, FOXA1 limited invasion/motility through regulation of genes linked to cell migration and epithelial–mesenchymal transition, and perturbing FOXA1 increased invasive phenotypes (Jin et al., 2013). Clinically oriented tissue analyses further reported that FOXA1 nuclear expression was associated with prostate cancer progression and that FOXA1 expression patterns characterized castration-resistant prostate cancer in patient samples (Gerhardt et al., 2012). In castration-resistant prostate cancer models, FOXA1 loss was shown to derepress TGF-β pathway output (including TGFB3) and pharmacologic TGF-β pathway inhibition suppressed progression in preclinical settings described in that study (Song et al., 2019). ([University of Helsinki][1])
