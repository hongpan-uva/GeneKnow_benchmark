## FOXA1 — prostate cancer

**Query:** What functional relationships between FOXA1 and prostate cancer have been directly reported in peer-reviewed research articles?

FOXA1 is a transcription factor that plays a crucial role in prostate cancer development and progression [1]. Research has revealed several functional relationships between FOXA1 and prostate cancer, highlighting its diverse roles in different stages of the disease [2]. FOXA1 has been shown to regulate alternative splicing in prostate cancer [4] and has a role in rewiring the alternative splicing landscape in prostate cancer through a cascade of events from chromatin access, to splicing factor regulation, and, finally, to alternative splicing of exons influencing patient survival [4]. The FOXA1 transcription factor (TF) pioneers androgen receptor (AR) chromatin binding in the prostate epithelium [6] and is reprogrammed to neuroendocrine-specific regulatory elements in neuroendocrine prostate cancer (NEPC) [7]. FOXA1 also regulates the expression of NSUN2, a RNA m5C methyltransferase, which enhances the stability of TRIM28 mRNA by adding m5C modification, promoting the expression of TRIM28 [8]. Furthermore, FOXA1 can be repressed by SETD7, a methyltransferase that negatively regulates the FOXA1-LSD1 axis [9]. In addition, FOXA1 has been shown to regulate the expression of other genes, including HNRNPK and SRSF1, which are involved in alternative splicing [4]. FOXA1 also regulates the expression of NSUN2, which is involved in the advancement of prostate cancer by preserving TRIM28 mRNA stability in a m5C-dependent manner [8]. Overall, FOXA1 plays a complex and multifaceted role in prostate cancer, and its regulation is crucial for the development and progression of the disease [0]. FOXA1 has been identified as a tumor suppressor in early prostate cancer [1]. In contrast, in advanced stages, FOXA1 acts as an oncogene [0]. FOXA1 promotes the development and progression of prostate cancer through various mechanisms, including the activation of the androgen receptor (AR) pathway [0][1], the regulation of alternative splicing [4], and the inhibition of cell motility and epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT) [1]. Furthermore, FOXA1 has been linked to the regulation of cell growth, proliferation, and apoptosis in prostate cancer cells [0][1]. It also plays a role in the development of hormone-independent prostate cancer, where FOXA1 promotes cell growth and survival through AR-independent mechanisms [1]. Additionally, FOXA1 has been shown to regulate the expression of genes involved in prostate cancer progression, such as the WNT pathway [0], and the epigenetic regulation of gene expression [8]. Moreover, FOXA1 has been implicated in the development of neuroendocrine prostate cancer (NEPC), a treatment-resistant form of prostate cancer [7]. In NEPC, FOXA1 has been reprogrammed to bind to new regulatory elements and maintain its expression, contributing to the disease's aggressiveness. In summary, FOXA1 plays a complex and multifaceted role in prostate cancer, acting as both a tumor suppressor and an oncogene depending on the stage of the disease [4]. Its interactions with other transcription factors, such as CREB1, and its involvement in the regulation of alternative splicing, cell growth, and survival, highlight its importance in prostate cancer development and progression [5]. FOXA1 can also regulate alternative splicing in prostate cancer [4] and is involved in the rewiring of the alternative splicing landscape in prostate cancer through a cascade of events from chromatin access, to splicing factor regulation, and, finally, to alternative splicing of exons influencing patient survival [4]. Additionally, FOXA1 is reprogrammed to neuroendocrine-specific regulatory elements in neuroendocrine prostate cancer (NEPC) and is required for proliferation and expression of NE lineage-defining genes in NEPC [7]. FOXA1-dependent NSUN2 facilitates the advancement of prostate cancer by preserving TRIM28 mRNA stability in a m5C-dependent manner [8]. Furthermore, SETD7 functions as a transcription repressor in prostate cancer via methylating FOXA1 [9]. Overall, FOXA1 plays a critical role in prostate cancer development and progression, and its dysregulation can lead to cancer progression [3].
