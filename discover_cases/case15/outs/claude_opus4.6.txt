FOXA1 (Forkhead Box A1) is a pioneer transcription factor that decompacts chromatin to enable androgen receptor (AR) binding to DNA, thereby governing AR-driven transcriptional programs in prostate epithelial cells (Parolia et al., 2019; Adams et al., 2019). FOXA1 expression is significantly higher in prostate cancer than in normal prostate tissue and correlates with PSA levels and Gleason score (Gerhardt et al., 2012). Depletion of FOXA1 in castration-resistant prostate cancer cell lines abrogates the oncogenic potential of AR variants, indicating a functional dependency of AR signaling on FOXA1 even in advanced disease (Robinson et al., 2015). Sequencing of 3,086 human prostate cancers identified FOXA1 mutations in approximately 11% of cases, with two hotspots in the forkhead domain—Wing2 (~50% of mutations) and R219 (~5%)—where Wing2 mutations appeared across all disease stages and R219 mutations were enriched in neuroendocrine histology (Adams et al., 2019). In a cohort of 1,546 prostate cancers, FOXA1 alterations were classified into three structural classes with a collective prevalence of 35%: Class 1 Wing2 mutations that transactivate a luminal AR program, Class 2 C-terminal truncations acquired in metastatic disease that promote WNT-driven metastasis, and Class 3 genomic rearrangements that reposition regulatory elements (Parolia et al., 2019). In vivo mouse models subsequently demonstrated that Class 1 mutations, combined with p53 loss, drive androgen-dependent adenocarcinoma, while Class 2 mutations reprogram luminal cells into a progenitor-like state enabling survival under castrate androgen levels (Eyunni et al., 2025).
