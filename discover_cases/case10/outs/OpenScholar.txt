## CEBPA — neutrophils or granulocytes

**Query:** What functional relationships between CEBPA and neutrophils or granulocytes have been directly reported in peer-reviewed research articles?

CEBPA is a key transcription factor involved in the regulation of granulopoiesis, and its dysregulation has been implicated in the development of acute myeloid leukemia (AML) [0][2]. Studies have shown that CEBPA is required for the formation of granulocyte-monocyte progenitors, but its role in subsequent myeloid lineage specification remains uncertain [0]. Higher levels of C/EBPα are required for granulocyte lineage specification, and lower levels for monocyte lineage specification, and this myeloid bifurcation may be facilitated by increased Cebpa gene expression in granulocyte compared to monocyte progenitors [0]. Furthermore, CEBPA has been shown to form a regulatory network with Gfi1aa and Lsd1, controlling neutrophil development and providing a disease progression traceable model for myelodysplasia syndrome [1]. Additionally, CEBPA has been identified as a target of the oncometabolite R-2HG, which inhibits leukemia cell proliferation and promotes cell cycle arrest and apoptosis [4]. In terms of functional relationships, CEBPA has been shown to promote the expression of genes involved in neutrophil differentiation, such as HK3 and KLF5 [2][4], and to regulate the stability of BCL-2, which is involved in cell survival and apoptosis [2][4][7]. CEBPA has also been shown to facilitate the transcription of LOXL2 and LOXL3, which are involved in cancer progression [7]. Overall, CEBPA plays a crucial role in the regulation of granulopoiesis and neutrophil development, and its dysregulation has been implicated in the development of AML and other diseases [0][1][2][4][7]. Moreover, CEBPA has been shown to be involved in the regulation of genes involved in granulopoiesis, such as Gfi1 and Klf5 [2], and to promote the AT2 program by recruiting the lung lineage TF NKX2-1 [5]. CEBPA also restricts AT2 cell plasticity in the mouse lung, and its temporal change in CEBPA-dependent plasticity reflects AT2 cell developmental history [5]. In summary, CEBPA is a critical transcription factor involved in the regulation of granulopoiesis and neutrophil development, and its dysregulation has been implicated in the development of AML and other diseases. Its functional relationships with neutrophils and granulocytes are complex and multifaceted, involving the regulation of genes involved in differentiation, survival, and cancer progression [2][4][7].
