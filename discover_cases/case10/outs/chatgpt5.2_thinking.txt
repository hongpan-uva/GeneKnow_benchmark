CEBPA (encoding the transcription factor C/EBPα) has been shown to be required for neutrophil development and neutrophil-lineage gene programs in multiple experimental systems. In mice with targeted disruption of Cebpa, investigators reported a selective block in neutrophil differentiation with an absence of mature neutrophils in blood and fetal liver, alongside a selective loss of granulocyte colony-stimulating factor receptor (G-CSFR/CSF3R) expression in hematopoietic tissues. ([PubMed][1]) In primary human cells and bipotential myeloid cell lines, C/EBPα expression was reported to be preferentially upregulated during granulocytic differentiation, and conditional induction of C/EBPα alone triggered neutrophilic differentiation with upregulation of G-CSFR and secondary granule protein genes; the same induction also blocked monocytic differentiation in their system. ([PubMed][2]) In vivo, a mouse germline study using C/EBPα alleles defective in E2F repression found these alleles failed to support granulocyte (including neutrophil granulocyte) differentiation, providing genetic evidence linking a specific C/EBPα function to granulopoiesis. ([PubMed][3]) In a model cell system engineered to separate G-CSFR signaling from C/EBPα level changes, G-CSFR signals and C/EBPα were reported to cooperate to induce expression of neutrophil-associated genes such as myeloperoxidase and neutrophil elastase. ([PubMed][4])
