CEBPA (CCAAT/enhancer-binding protein alpha) is a leucine zipper transcription factor that serves as a master regulator of neutrophil development. Mice with targeted disruption of the CEBPA gene exhibit a selective block in neutrophil differentiation, with no mature neutrophils or eosinophils observed in the blood or fetal liver, while other hematopoietic lineages such as monocytes remain unaffected (Zhang et al., 1997). Conditional expression of C/EBPα is sufficient to trigger neutrophilic differentiation, and it functions as a regulatory switch capable of inducing granulocytic development from bipotential myeloid progenitors (Radomska et al., 1998; Pabst et al., 2001). Conditional knockout of CEBPA in adult mouse bone marrow results in failed generation of granulocyte-monocyte progenitors and a complete block of neutrophilic development at the common myeloid progenitor stage (Zhang et al., 2004, as reviewed in Avouch et al., 2017). CEBPA-deficient progenitors also exhibit selective loss of granulocyte colony-stimulating factor receptor expression, rendering them unable to respond to G-CSF signaling (Zhang et al., 1997). A myeloid-specific enhancer located +42 kb from CEBPA is essential for its expression in hematopoietic stem cells and myeloid progenitors; germline deletion of this enhancer in mice leads to severe defects in the granulocytic lineage (Avellino et al., 2016). Homozygous deletion of the murine +37 kb enhancer completely abrogates neutrophil production, causing neutropenia in blood and bone marrow and secondarily depleting long-term hematopoietic stem cells (Avellino et al., 2022). Dominant-negative CEBPA mutations have been identified in acute myeloid leukemia patients, where they block C/EBPα DNA binding and transactivation of granulocyte target genes, inhibiting granulocytic differentiation (Pabst et al., 2001).
