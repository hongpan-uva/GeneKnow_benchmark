TCF7L2 (Transcription Factor 7-Like 2) encodes a transcription factor in the Wnt signaling pathway that has been consistently identified as the strongest common genetic risk factor for type 2 diabetes mellitus (T2DM). A landmark genome-wide association study by Grant et al. (2006) first identified variants in TCF7L2 (notably rs7903146) as conferring significant risk for T2DM, with the T allele of rs7903146 associated with an approximately 1.4-fold increased risk per allele. Subsequent functional studies demonstrated that TCF7L2 plays a critical role in pancreatic beta-cell function; Lyssenko et al. (2007) reported that carriers of TCF7L2 risk alleles exhibited impaired insulin secretion and enhanced rates of progression from impaired glucose tolerance to overt diabetes in prospective cohorts. Mechanistically, TCF7L2 has been shown to regulate the transcription of proglucagon and thereby influence incretin hormone (GLP-1) production in enteroendocrine cells, as demonstrated by Yi et al. (2005), linking it to the incretin axis relevant to glucose homeostasis. Additionally, a meta-analysis by Cauchi et al. (2007) confirmed the robust and reproducible association of TCF7L2 rs7903146 with T2DM risk across multiple ethnic populations, establishing it as the most replicated T2DM susceptibility locus identified to date.
