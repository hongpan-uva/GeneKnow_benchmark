KLF4 (Krüppel-like factor 4) functions predominantly as a tumor suppressor in colorectal cancer (CRC). KLF4 mRNA levels in a panel of 30 CRC specimens were approximately 52% of those in paired normal colonic tissues, and multiple mechanisms of KLF4 inactivation were identified, including loss of heterozygosity at the KLF4 locus on chromosome 9q, promoter hypermethylation, and point mutations that diminished its ability to activate the p21^(WAF1/Cip1) promoter (Zhao et al., 2004). Mechanistically, KLF4 suppresses CRC cell proliferation by upregulating p21^(WAF1/Cip1) and downregulating cyclin D1, and it also transcriptionally activates the tumor suppressor NDRG2; KLF4-mediated inhibition of CRC cell proliferation and tumorigenesis was shown to be dependent on NDRG2 signaling in both cell-based and xenograft mouse models (Ma et al., 2017). In a murine model, intestinal epithelium–specific deletion of Klf4 led to significantly more adenomatous polyps and carcinomas in situ following azoxymethane/dextran sodium sulfate treatment, accompanied by increased genetic instability including centrosome amplification (Yang et al., 2018). Loss of KLF4 expression in both human CRC patient specimens and mouse colitis-associated cancer tissues was associated with markers of epithelial-mesenchymal transition, and overexpression of KLF4 in CRC cells decreased mesenchymal EMT markers such as ZEB1, SNAI1, and SNAI2 (Agbo et al., 2019). In a tissue microarray study of 367 colon cancer specimens, KLF4 levels were significantly lower in tumor versus noncancer tissues, and KLF4 was identified as an independent predictor of overall survival and disease-free survival (Patel et al., 2010).
