Programmed Death-Ligand 1 (**PD-L1**) is a transmembrane protein that facilitates immune evasion in non-small cell lung cancer (**NSCLC**) by binding to the Programmed Cell Death Protein 1 (**PD-1**) receptor on tumor-infiltrating T lymphocytes (Vivaldi et al., 2026). This interaction triggers the recruitment of the phosphatase **SHP2**, which dephosphorylates the **CD28** costimulatory pathway and inhibits T-cell receptor-mediated signaling, resulting in functional T-cell deactivation (Vivaldi et al., 2026). In the tumor microenvironment, PD-L1 expression is dynamically upregulated as an adaptive immune resistance mechanism in response to **interferon-gamma** (IFN-$\gamma$) via the **JAK/STAT1** signaling pathway (He et al., 2019). Additionally, intrinsic oncogenic drivers, such as **EGFR** mutations and **ALK** rearrangements, can constitutively induce PD-L1 overexpression through downstream signaling pathways including **MAPK** and **PI3K/mTOR** (Ricciuti et al., 2020). Clinically, the PD-L1 **Tumor Proportion Score (TPS)** serves as a predictive biomarker; NSCLC patients with a TPS $\ge$ 50% demonstrate significantly improved progression-free and overall survival when treated with pembrolizumab monotherapy compared to standard chemotherapy (Reck et al., 2016). However, the presence of high PD-L1 in tumors with co-occurring EGFR or ALK alterations does not typically equate to high sensitivity to immune checkpoint inhibitors (Mao et al., 2026).
