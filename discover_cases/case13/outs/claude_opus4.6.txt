PD-L1 (programmed death-ligand 1) is a transmembrane protein that binds to PD-1 on T cells, thereby inhibiting T-cell-mediated immune responses against tumor cells, a mechanism exploited by many cancers including NSCLC. Tumor PD-L1 expression is observed in approximately 50–70% of NSCLC cases and has been adopted as a predictive biomarker for response to immune checkpoint inhibitor (ICI) therapy (Reck et al., 2016). The KEYNOTE-024 trial demonstrated that pembrolizumab monotherapy significantly improved progression-free survival compared to platinum-based chemotherapy in previously untreated NSCLC patients whose tumors had a PD-L1 tumor proportion score (TPS) of 50% or greater (Reck et al., 2016). The KEYNOTE-042 trial extended this finding, showing an overall survival benefit with pembrolizumab in patients with TPS ≥1% (Mok et al., 2019). However, PD-L1 expression is an imperfect biomarker, as some PD-L1–negative patients still respond to ICIs and some PD-L1–high patients do not, indicating that additional factors such as tumor mutational burden influence treatment outcomes (Hellmann et al., 2018). PD-L1 upregulation in NSCLC is driven by both intrinsic oncogenic signaling pathways (e.g., EGFR mutations, ALK rearrangements) and extrinsic interferon-gamma signaling within the tumor microenvironment (Akbay et al., 2013).
