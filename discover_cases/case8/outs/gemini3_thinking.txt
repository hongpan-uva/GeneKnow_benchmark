RUNX2 (Runt-related transcription factor 2) is the primary determinant of the osteoblast lineage, essential for the commitment of mesenchymal stem cells to an osteogenic fate. Genetic deletion of *Runx2* in mice results in a complete absence of osteoblasts and a skeleton consisting entirely of cartilage (Komori et al., 1997; Otto et al., 1997). RUNX2 functions by binding to the Osteoblast-Specific Element 2 (OSE2) in the promoter regions of key bone matrix genes, such as *Osteocalcin* (*Bglap*), *Type I Collagen* (*Col1a1*), and *Bone Sialoprotein* (*Ibsp*), thereby driving their expression (Ducy et al., 1997). It also serves as a scaffold within the nucleus, organizing the subnuclear distribution of transcriptional machinery required for osteoblast-specific gene expression (Stein et al., 2004). While necessary for early differentiation, RUNX2 must be downregulated for the final maturation of osteoblasts into osteocytes; sustained high levels of RUNX2 actually inhibit terminal maturation and lead to osteopenia (Liu et al., 2001). Furthermore, its transcriptional activity is stabilized by the co-factor $Cbf-\beta$, which is required for RUNX2 to effectively induce bone formation in vivo (Yoshida et al., 2002).
