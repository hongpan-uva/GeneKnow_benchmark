NEUROD1 (also called NeuroD) is a basic helix–loop–helix transcription factor that, when ectopically expressed in *Xenopus* embryos, can convert non-neuronal ectoderm into neurons that display neuronal morphology and marker expression, demonstrating its capacity to drive neuronal differentiation in vivo (Lee et al., 1995). In mice, NeuroD is required for normal differentiation of granule neurons in the cerebellum and hippocampus: NeuroD-null animals show severely reduced granule-cell differentiation and increased death of postmitotic granule cells, and NeuroD was concluded to regulate expression of differentiation genes needed for postmitotic granule-cell survival (Miyata et al., 1999). In the adult mouse brain, inducible, neural stem/progenitor-targeted deletion of *Neurod1* results in substantially fewer adult-born neurons in the hippocampus and olfactory bulb, with evidence of impaired survival/maturation of newborn neurons, supporting a cell-intrinsic requirement for NeuroD1 during adult neurogenesis (Gao et al., 2009). In vivo gene delivery studies further show that forced NEUROD1 expression in astrocytes can induce astrocyte-to-neuron conversion under non-injury (non-reactive) conditions in the mouse brain (Brulet et al., 2017).
