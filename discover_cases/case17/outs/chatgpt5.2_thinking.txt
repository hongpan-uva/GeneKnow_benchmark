The APOE ε4 allele (encoding the apoE4 protein isoform) is associated with increased risk and earlier age at onset of late-onset Alzheimer’s disease (AD) in family-based analyses showing a gene-dose relationship between ε4 copy number, AD risk, and mean onset age (Corder et al., 1993). In late-onset familial AD, ε4 was reported at increased frequency relative to controls in the same study that also demonstrated high-avidity binding of apolipoprotein E to synthetic β-amyloid (Aβ) in vitro (Strittmatter et al., 1993a), and a separate in vitro study reported isoform-specific effects of apoE binding to synthetic Aβ peptide (Strittmatter et al., 1993b). In humans, metabolic labeling studies measuring Aβ kinetics in vivo reported that apoE isoforms differentially regulate brain Aβ clearance, and interpreted these findings as evidence that APOE alleles can influence AD risk via differential Aβ clearance (Castellano et al., 2011). In a PDAPP transgenic mouse model expressing human APOE isoforms, apoE isoform–dependent differences in brain Aβ levels were reported (Bales et al., 2009). In living human APOE4 carriers, neuroimaging and cerebrospinal fluid measures were used to report that APOE4 is associated with blood–brain barrier dysfunction and that this dysfunction predicted cognitive decline independent of amyloid-β and tau status in the studied cohort (Montagne et al., 2020).
