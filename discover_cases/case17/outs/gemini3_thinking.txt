The functional relationship between **APOE4** and Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is characterized by a multifactorial "hit" model involving protein aggregation, neurovascular dysfunction, and metabolic failure. The $\epsilon 4$ allele is the most significant genetic risk factor for late-onset AD, with recent comprehensive modeling indicating that *APOE* variants contribute to the development of the vast majority of all cases (Williams et al., 2026). Functionally, the APOE4 protein isoform impairs the proteolytic clearance of amyloid-beta (Aβ) and directly promotes its fibrillar aggregation into neurotoxic plaques (Castellano et al., 2011). Beyond amyloid, APOE4 exacerbates tau-mediated neurodegeneration by driving microglia into a chronic pro-inflammatory state and inducing a systemic proteomic signature that correlates with neuroinflammation across the brain (Shi et al., 2017; Shvetcov et al., 2025). Furthermore, APOE4 triggers a pro-inflammatory cyclophilin A–matrix metalloproteinase-9 pathway in pericytes, leading to blood-brain barrier breakdown (Bell et al., 2012). At the cellular level, the variant disrupts glial lipid homeostasis and blocks neuronal receptors required for lipid uptake, thereby preventing neurons from utilizing fatty acids as an alternative energy source during age-related glucose scarcity (Sienski et al., 2021; Greda et al., 2025). Currently, no direct evidence exists to definitively classify APOE4 as exclusively a "gain-of-toxic-function" or "loss-of-physiological-function" variant; rather, it appears to operate through a synergy of both mechanisms (Shvetcov et al., 2025).
