## APOE4 — Alzheimer's disease (AD)

**Query:** What functional relationships between APOE4 (APOE ε4) and Alzheimer's disease (AD) have been directly reported in peer-reviewed research articles?

APOE4 (APOE ε4) is a significant risk factor for the development of Alzheimer's disease (AD) [1][3][5][6]. Studies have identified various functional relationships between APOE4 and AD, which can be grouped into several key areas: (1) altered cholesterol metabolism, (2) myelination and neuronal support, (3) microglial function and neuroinflammation, and (4) cognitive and pathological heterogeneity. 1. Altered cholesterol metabolism: APOE4 has been shown to impair myelination in the brain by affecting cholesterol homeostasis and transport [0]. Cholesterol is crucial for the proper functioning of neurons and oligodendrocytes, and its dysregulation can lead to reduced myelination, which is associated with AD [0]. Moreover, APOE4 has been linked to changes in cholesterol metabolism, with studies suggesting that APOE4 carriers have altered cholesterol profiles in the brain and blood [8]. 2. Myelination and neuronal support: APOE4 has been found to have a negative impact on myelination in the brain, particularly in oligodendrocytes [0]. Myelination is essential for neuronal function and communication, and its impairment can contribute to cognitive decline [0]. Additionally, APOE4 has been shown to affect other glial cells, such as astrocytes, which play a crucial role in maintaining neuronal health and function [3]. 3. Microglial function and neuroinflammation: APOE4 has been linked to impaired microglial function, which is critical for maintaining brain health and responding to neurodegeneration [3][5]. Microglia are the brain's resident immune cells, and their dysfunction can lead to neuroinflammation, which is a hallmark of AD [3]. APOE4 has been shown to modulate microglial activity, promoting a pro-inflammatory state and impairing phagocytic function [3]. This can lead to the accumulation of amyloid-beta peptides, exacerbating neuroinflammation and neuronal damage [3]. 4. Cognitive and pathological heterogeneity: APOE4 has been associated with cognitive and pathological differences between APOE4-positive (APOE4+) and APOE4-negative (APOE4-) AD patients [6]. APOE4+ AD patients tend to have more tau accumulation and brain atrophy in the medial temporal lobe, resulting in greater memory impairment, compared to APOE4- AD patients [6]. On the other hand, APOE4- AD patients tend to have more tau accumulation and brain atrophy in the frontal and parietal lobes, resulting in greater impairment in executive function, visuospatial abilities, and language, compared to APOE4+ AD patients [6]. Understanding the APOE4-AD relationship can provide valuable insights into the heterogeneity of AD, which may have important implications for therapeutic treatment and personalized medicine [6]. APOE4 may also be linked to other conditions, such as periodontal disease (PD), through shared genetic variants, inflammatory pathways, and dyslipidemia, involving both peripheral and central pathways [7]. Furthermore, APOE4 has been associated with lower levels of C-reactive protein (CRP) in the blood across all age groups, and this inverse correlation may be related to the accumulation of low-grade inflammatory stress throughout life [8]. In summary, APOE4 is a significant risk factor for AD, and its functional relationships with the disease involve altered cholesterol metabolism, myelination and neuronal support, microglial function and neuroinflammation, and cognitive and pathological heterogeneity. Further research is needed to understand these relationships and to identify potential therapeutic targets for AD treatment.
