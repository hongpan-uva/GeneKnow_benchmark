The apolipoprotein E ε4 (APOE4) allele is the strongest known genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer's disease, with heterozygous carriers facing approximately three-fold increased risk and homozygous carriers facing approximately 12-fold increased risk compared to APOE3 homozygotes (Farrer et al., 1997). APOE4 is associated with earlier onset of AD symptoms in a dose-dependent manner (Corder et al., 1993). Mechanistically, APOE4 promotes amyloid-β (Aβ) aggregation and impairs its clearance from the brain, leading to increased amyloid plaque burden (Castellano et al., 2011). APOE4 also contributes to blood-brain barrier dysfunction and cerebrovascular impairment, which may independently accelerate neurodegeneration (Bell et al., 2012). Additionally, APOE4 is associated with increased neuroinflammation through enhanced microglial activation and proinflammatory cytokine release (Rodriguez et al., 2014). APOE4 carriers exhibit reduced cerebral glucose metabolism in AD-vulnerable brain regions even prior to cognitive decline, suggesting early metabolic disruption (Reiman et al., 2004). The allele also impairs synaptic plasticity and dendritic spine maintenance through disrupted lipid transport and cholesterol homeostasis in neurons (Ji et al., 2003).
