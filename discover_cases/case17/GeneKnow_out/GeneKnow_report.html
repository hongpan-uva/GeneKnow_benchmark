APOE4

APOE4 is the strongest genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer’s disease [1-3]. APOE4 modulates lipid metabolism [3,4]. APOE4 modulates glial innate immune responses [3-5]. Specifically, APOE4 is the major genetic risk factor for Alzheimer’s disease and translocates to the nucleus to regulate genes for Aβ and inflammation [1]. APOE4 interacts with mitochondria and causes dysfunction with increased Ca2+ and reactive oxygen species [1]. The mitochondrial dysfunction relates to neurodegeneration [1]. In neurons, APOE4 increased synapse number and elevated Aβ42 secretion [4]. In astrocytes, APOE4 impaired Aβ uptake and caused cholesterol accumulation [4]. APOE4 in microglia-like cells altered morphology [4]. The altered morphology correlated with reduced Aβ phagocytosis [4]. APOE4 is associated with increased age-dependent terminally inflammatory microglia (TIMs) in AD models [5]. TIMs concomitantly express inflammatory and cell-intrinsic stress markers and shift toward stress-marker–high, effector-lo states [5]. The defective amyloid-β clearance suggests contribution to AD risk [5]. ApoE4 is the strongest genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer’s disease and accelerates progression [2]. ApoE4 can initiate and accelerate Aβ accumulation and increase Aβ oligomers [2]. ApoE4 affects tau phosphorylation [2]. Therapeutic targeting of ApoE4 can slow AD progression [2]. APOE4 is the strongest genetic risk factor for late-onset Alzheimer’s disease [3]. APOE4 modulates lipid metabolism and glial innate immune responses [3]. APOE4 associates with earlier amyloid-β deposition, faster progression, and increased medial temporal and CSF tau in AD [3].
