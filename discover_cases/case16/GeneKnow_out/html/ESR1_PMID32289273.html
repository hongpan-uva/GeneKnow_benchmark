Article Summary

ESR1 amplifications and in‑frame gene fusions occur in ER+ breast cancers, and ESR1 in‑frame gene fusions have been found in at least 1% of metastatic breast cancers; they usually occur in tumors resistant to endocrine therapy. Most ESR1 fusions retain the N-terminal DNA-binding domain and exclude the ER ligand-binding domain by fusing to diverse C-terminal partner genes. These fusion proteins drive estrogen-independent ER transcriptional activity and promote cell proliferation, cell motility, and metastasis. Breast cancer cells and a patient-derived xenograft expressing these fusions remained sensitive to CDK4/6 inhibition, suggesting that adding CDK4/6 inhibitors to adjuvant endocrine therapy may prevent acquisition of these fusions. In ER-positive breast cancers, ESR1 ligand-binding-domain mutations such as Y537S drive estrogen-independent metabolic rewiring. ESR1 Y537S-expressing cells exhibit elevated tricarboxylic acid cycle activity, glucose independence, and reliance on glutamine as an alternative carbon source. Antiestrogens suppressed ER and induced a metabolically dormant, self‑renewal‑deficient state in tumors. Chronic fulvestrant exposure increased paracrine IL‑6. Paracrine IL‑6 elevated oxidative phosphorylation to restore growth in quiescent CD133hi/ERlo/Mito lo cells in an ER‑independent manner. PROTACs are hetero-bifunctional small molecules that recruit an E3-ubiquitin ligase to the target protein and have the potential to degrade both wild-type and mutant ESR1 by binding to a region shared between mutant and wild-type ER. The PROTAC ARV‑471 degrades Y537S and D538G mutants and inhibits tumor growth in a patient‑derived xenograft harboring ESR1 Y537S. The authors speculate that agents targeting both mutant and wild‑type ER earlier in treatment may prevent acquisition of ligand‑binding domain mutations.

ESR1 amplifications and gene fusions have been found in ER + breast cancers, albeit at much lower frequencies than point mutations ( Lei et al., 2019 ). Whether ESR1 amplification is associated with resistance to endocrine therapy remains unclear. Stable ESR1 in-frame gene fusions were first reported in 2013 ( Li et al., 2013 ) and have since been found in at least 1% of MBCs (usually in tumors resistant to endocrine therapy) ( Hartmaier et al., 2018 ), although the frequency is likely higher considering capture-based targeted exon sequencing often fails to detect fusions. Most ESR1 gene fusions retain the N-terminal DNA binding domain of the ER fused to diverse C-terminal partner genes, thereby excluding the ER LBD. Lei et al. showed that these fusion proteins drive estrogen-independent ER transcriptional activity, cell proliferation, cell motility, and metastasis. Breast cancer cells and a PDX expressing these fusion products remained sensitive to CDK4/6 inhibition ( Lei et al., 2018 ), suggesting that addition of CDK4/6i to endocrine therapy in the adjuvant setting may prevent acquisition of these fusions.

ER + breast cancers have been shown to evade endocrine therapy by switching to alternate metabolic processes. For example, the aggressive nature of ESR1 LBD mutations was attributed to estrogen-independent metabolic rewiring. Compared to breast cancer cells expressing WT ESR1, which are mostly glucose dependent even upon estrogen stimulation, ESR1 Y537S -expressing cells exhibit elevated tricarboxylic acid cycle activity, glucose independence, and reliance on glutamine as an alternative carbon source ( Zinger et al., 2019 ). Sansone et al. performed molecular profiling of pre- and on-treatment tumors from patients in a clinical trial of neoadjuvant letrozole, revealing an inverse correlation between ER and expression of the CSC marker CD133. Mechanistically, antiestrogens induced a metabolically-dormant, self-renewal-deficient state in which ER expression was suppressed. However, chronic exposure to fulvestrant increased paracrine expression of IL-6, which in turn elevated oxidative phosphorylation to restore growth in quiescent CD133 hi /ER lo /Mito lo cells, in an ER-independent manner ( Sansone et al., 2016 ).
In breast cancer, proteolysis targeting chimeras (PROTACs) can degrade both wild-type and mutant ESR1. They recruit an E3-ubiquitin ligase and bind a region shared between mutant and wild-type ER to induce degradation. For example, the PROTAC ARV-471 degrades the Y537S and D538G mutants and inhibits tumor growth in a patient-derived xenograft harboring ESR1 Y537S. The authors speculate that agents targeting both mutant and wild-type ER earlier in treatment may prevent acquisition of ligand-binding domain mutations.

Proteolysis targeting chimeras (PROTACs), hetero-bifunctional small molecules that recruit an E3-ubiquiting ligase to the target protein, have the potential to degrade both WT and mutant ER by binding to a region that is shared between mutant and WT ( Figure 1E ). For example, the PROTAC ARV-471 degrades the Y537S and D538G mutants and inhibits tumor growth in a patient-derived xenograft (PDX) harboring ESR1 Y537S ( Flanagan et al., 2019 ). We speculate that use of agents that target both mutant and WT ER earlier in the course of breast cancer treatment may prevent the acquisition of LBD mutations.
