## ESR1 — breast cancer

**Query:** What functional relationships between ESR1 and breast cancer have been directly reported in peer-reviewed research articles?

ESR1 mutations are a common mechanism of acquired resistance to endocrine therapy in hormone receptor-positive (HR+) breast cancer. These mutations can preexist in primary tumors and be enriched during metastasis [3]. ESR1 mutations can disrupt the ESR1 gene, leading to a reprogrammed cell adhesive gene network, enhanced cell-cell contacts, and decreased cell-extracellular matrix (ECM) adhesion [0]. This can result in the formation of larger, more compact circulating tumor cell (CTC) clusters, which are enriched in patients with ESR1-mutated metastatic breast cancer [0]. ESR1 mutations can also lead to the upregulation of genes involved in cell adhesion and migration, such as SLC7A11, and downregulation of genes involved in cell adhesion and migration, such as TIMP3 [0]. ESR1 mutations are associated with poor clinical outcomes in HR+ breast cancer [2][3], and patients with ESR1 mutations can benefit from treatment with fulvestrant and CDK4/6-targeted therapies [3]. ESR1 mutations can also lead to the activation of the PI3K/AKT pathway, which is a common mechanism of resistance to endocrine therapy [8]. Furthermore, ESR1 mutations can disrupt the immune surveillance in HR+ breast cancer, leading to the downregulation of molecules involved in antigen presentation and loss of HLA Class I gene expression [2]. In addition, ESR1 mutations can be associated with the development of metastasis, and the presence of ESR1 mutations in circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) can be used as a biomarker for metastatic disease [3]. ESR1 mutations can also be used to guide treatment decisions, with patients with ESR1 mutations deriving clinical benefit from treatment with fulvestrant and CDK4/6-targeted therapies [3]. Overall, the functional relationships between ESR1 and breast cancer are complex and multifaceted, and further research is needed to fully understand the mechanisms by which ESR1 mutations contribute to the development and progression of breast cancer.
