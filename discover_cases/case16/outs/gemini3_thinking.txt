The *ESR1* gene encodes estrogen receptor $\alpha$ (ER$\alpha$), a ligand-activated transcription factor that serves as the primary driver of tumor growth in approximately 70% of breast cancer cases (Characterization of ESR1 alterations, 2026). While *ESR1* mutations are present in fewer than 1% of primary, treatment-naive tumors, they occur in 20% to 40% of metastatic ER-positive cases following selective pressure from endocrine therapy (Jeselsohn et al., 2015). Specific hotspot mutations within the ligand-binding domain, notably Y537S and D538G, result in constitutive, ligand-independent ER$\alpha$ activity that confers resistance to aromatase inhibitors by bypassing the need for estrogen (Robinson et al., 2013; Toy et al., 2013). Furthermore, recurrent *ESR1* gene fusions—most frequently involving exons 1 through 6—promote endocrine resistance and metastatic progression by inducing estrogen-independent growth and epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT) transcriptional programs (Lei et al., 2018). These genomic alterations collectively enable tumor cells to maintain oncogenic signaling despite estrogen deprivation, facilitating disease recurrence and therapeutic failure.
