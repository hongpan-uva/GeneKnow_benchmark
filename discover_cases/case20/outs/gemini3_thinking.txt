The $t(2;13)(q35;q14)$ chromosomal translocation generates the *PAX3-FOXO1* fusion gene, which acts as the primary oncogenic driver in alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma (ARMS) (Nguyen & Barr, 2018; Olanich & Barr, 2013). This chimeric transcription factor incorporates the DNA-binding domains of PAX3 and the transactivation domain of FOXO1 to function as a potent transcriptional activator (Manceau et al., 2021). Mechanistically, PAX3-FOXO1 recruits co-activators such as p300 and BRD4 to establish de novo super-enhancers, which drive high-level expression of genes regulating cell survival and proliferation (Chin et al., 2023; Nguyen & Barr, 2018). The fusion protein also globally alters the microRNA landscape and interferes with normal FOXO-mediated tumor suppression, effectively blocking terminal myogenic differentiation while maintaining cells in a proliferative state (Loupe et al., 2017; Schmitt-Ney & Camussi, 2015). Although essential for maintaining the transformed phenotype and tumor viability, experimental evidence indicates that PAX3-FOXO1 typically requires cooperating genetic lesions, such as *MYCN* overexpression or *CDKN2A* inactivation, to achieve full oncogenesis (Nguyen & Barr, 2018; Olanich & Barr, 2013). While various myogenic and mesenchymal progenitors have been proposed, no direct consensus currently exists regarding the definitive cell of origin for this malignancy (Jothi et al., 2012).
