In alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma (aRMS), a recurrent chromosomal translocation t(2;13)(q35;q14) creates a **PAX3–FOXO1** (historically PAX3–FKHR) fusion gene that is reported as a characteristic molecular alteration of this tumor subtype. (Galili et al., 1993) The resulting fusion protein retains the PAX3 DNA-binding domains fused to FOXO1-derived C-terminal sequences and has been experimentally shown to function as a **more potent transcriptional activator than wild-type PAX3** in reporter-based assays. (Fredericks et al., 1995) In a human myoblast-based model engineered for conditional PAX3–FOXO1 expression, PAX3–FOXO1 expression was reported to be **required for tumor initiation and for maintenance of established tumors**, while recurrent tumors could emerge without continued PAX3–FOXO1 expression (“not recurrence”) in that system. (Pandey et al., 2017) Mechanistically, PAX3–FOXO1 was reported to **upregulate RASSF4** in PAX3–FOXO1–positive aRMS cells and tumors; RASSF4 knockdown impaired proliferation/senescence evasion and reduced tumorigenesis, with evidence that RASSF4 can inhibit MST1/Hippo pathway signaling in this context. (Crose et al., 2014) Clinically, in a Children’s Oncology Group report, **PAX–FOXO1 fusion status** was prospectively associated with **unfavorable outcome** and was concluded to improve risk stratification for rhabdomyosarcoma. (Skapek et al., 2013)
