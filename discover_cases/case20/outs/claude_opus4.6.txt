PAX3-FOXO1 is a chimeric transcription factor generated by the t(2;13)(q35;q14) chromosomal translocation, which fuses the paired box and homeodomain DNA-binding domains of PAX3 to the transactivation domain of FOXO1, and is the defining molecular hallmark of fusion-positive alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma (ARMS) (Barr, 1998). The fusion protein functions as an aberrantly potent transcriptional activator compared to wild-type PAX3, driving expression of downstream target genes including MYCN, MET, and CXCR4 that promote cell survival, proliferation, and metastasis (Cao et al., 2010). PAX3-FOXO1 expression is associated with a more aggressive clinical phenotype and poorer prognosis relative to fusion-negative rhabdomyosarcoma, even after accounting for stage and histology (Sorensen et al., 2002). The fusion protein inhibits myogenic differentiation, maintaining tumor cells in a proliferative, undifferentiated state (Kikuchi et al., 2014). Additionally, PAX3-FOXO1 has been shown to epigenetically reprogram the chromatin landscape by establishing super-enhancers at loci critical for tumor maintenance (Gryder et al., 2017). Efforts to therapeutically target this fusion oncoprotein remain challenging due to its nature as a transcription factor, though approaches targeting its downstream effectors and epigenetic dependencies are under active investigation.
