Article Summary

PAX3-FOXO1 arises from the t(2;13)(q35;q14) translocation and is a gain-of-function transcription factor fusion protein. It is the initiating and defining feature of a subset of highly aggressive pediatric alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma (aRMS). In rhabdomyosarcoma, PAX3-FOXO1 is continuously required to maintain expression of genes that block terminal differentiation and sustain cell viability, including RUNX2, KLF4, PRDM12, FGF8, and FGFR2. Proximity labeling of endogenous PAX3-FOXO1 identified DNA-binding factors and SWI/SNF components such as ARID1A. The proximity-labeling data suggest recruitment of SWI/SNF may be required to maintain nucleosome-free regions at enhancers to allow full transcriptional complex assembly. PAX3 bound at the same loci was not sufficient to maintain open chromatin. The data indicate PAX3-FOXO1 contains neomorphic activities. The low somatic mutational burden in these tumors suggests PAX3-FOXO1 is the critical driver and an ideal therapeutic target, and understanding its function is therefore critical to define aRMS etiology and to develop more innovative therapeutic strategies.

By engineering the endogenous PAX3-FOXO1 for proximity labeling, we identified DNA binding factors and transcriptional complexes that associated with PAX3-FOXO1 ( Figure 4 ). Among the identified interacting proteins were components of the SWI/SNF complex such as ARID1A, which could suggest that the continued recruitment of SWI/SNF may be required to maintain the nucleosome free region at enhancers to allow full transcriptional complex assembly 45 . Our data also suggests that PAX3, which was bound at these same loci, was not sufficient to maintain open chromatin at these regulatory elements. These data indicate that PAX3-FOXO1, contains neomorphic activities; however, future studies are needed to more rigorously define these functions. Finally, these data indicate that PAX3-FOXO1 is continuously required to maintain the expression of genes critical for blocking terminal differentiation and to maintain cell viability such as RUNX2 , KLF4 , PRDM12 , FGF8 , and FGFR2 and further emphasize the utility of PAX3-FOXO1 as a therapeutic target in rhabdomyosarcoma.

Master transcription factors establish gene expression networks to regulate cell fate decisions, and their deregulation is associated with cancer development. One common way that transcription factor activity is altered in cancer is through chromosomal translocation that gives rise to gain-of-function transcription factor fusion proteins. One example, PAX3-FOXO1, arises from the t(2;13)(q35;q14) translocation, and is the initiating and defining feature of a subset of highly aggressive, pediatric alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma (aRMS) 1 – 5 . In addition to the t(2;13) translocation, the mutational burden of these cancers is quite low, averaging only 6.4 somatic mutations per tumor 6 , 7 . This suggests that PAX3-FOXO1 is the critical driver of these tumors and an ideal therapeutic target. Thus, a thorough understanding of PAX3-FOXO1 function is critical to define aRMS etiology as well as to develop more innovative therapeutic strategies for this aggressive disease.
