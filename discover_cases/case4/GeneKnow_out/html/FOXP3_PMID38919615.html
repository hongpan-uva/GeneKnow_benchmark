Article Summary

FOXP3+ regulatory T cells produce FOXP3 and exert immunosuppressive effects that regulate immunity and tumor biology. The cells mature in the thymus as natural regulatory T cells and TGF‑β can induce other T cells to become induced regulatory T cells. Post‑translational modifications are essential for mature FOXP3 function and numerous non‑coding RNAs have been implicated in activating FOXP3 signaling. In gastrointestinal malignancies FOXP3 expression, altered glucose metabolism, and a hypoxic tumor microenvironment influence FOXP3+ Treg function and these mechanisms have been suggested as potential therapeutic targets. In non‑small cell lung cancer FOXP3 marks increased tumor‑infiltrating Tregs compared with normal tissue. Overexpression of FOXP3 in Treg cells in vitro enhances immune‑cell activity and invasiveness, and an increase in FOXP3 levels in the tumor microenvironment may promote tumor cell growth. Advanced NSCLC patients have increased circulating CD4+CD25+FOXP3+ Tregs and their frequency correlates negatively with IL‑17 and positively with IL‑10. In cervical cancer FOXP3 coexpression with VISTA associates with clinical stage and double positivity predicts the worst prognosis. In breast cancer FOXP3 and HAT1 epigenetically enable CCR4 activation in CCR4+ Tregs and FOXP3 overexpression increases CCR4+ Treg infiltration. CCR4+ Treg infiltration is linked to reduced anti‑tumor immunity and tumor progression.

Foxp3+ regulatory T cells (Foxp3+ Treg) play a role in regulating various types of tumors, but uncertainty still exists regarding the exact mechanism underlying Foxp3+ Treg activation in gastrointestinal malignancies. As of now, research has shown that Foxp3+ Treg expression, altered glucose metabolism, or a hypoxic tumor microenvironment all affect Foxp3+ Treg function in the bodies of tumor patients. Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that post-translational modifications are essential for mature Foxp3 to function properly. Additionally, a considerable number of non-coding RNAs (ncRNAs) have been implicated in the activation of the Foxp3 signaling pathway. These mechanisms regulating Foxp3 may one day serve as potential therapeutic targets for gastrointestinal malignancies. This review primarily focuses on the properties and capabilities of Foxp3 and Foxp3+Treg. It emphasizes the advancement of research on the regulatory mechanisms of Foxp3 in different malignant tumors of the digestive system, providing new insights for the exploration of anticancer treatments.

Foxp3+ regulatory T (Treg) cells predominantly produce Foxp3 and regulate the body's immune system by exerting immunosuppressive effects. They arise by maturation in the thymus with release into the periphery, where they are termed natural regulatory T cells (nTregs). They can also be generated from other T cells by transforming growth factor-β (TGF-β) induction as induced regulatory T cells (iTregs). These cells protect self-reactive lymphocytes from immunological reactions and thereby help maintain immunity and tissue stability.
