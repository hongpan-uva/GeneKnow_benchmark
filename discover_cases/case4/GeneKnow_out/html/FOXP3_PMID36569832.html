Article Summary

FOXP3 is the lineage-determining transcription factor for regulatory T cells. FOXP3 forms a large molecular complex with multiple transcription factors and enzymatic subunits to dynamically regulate the development and function of regulatory T cells. FOXP3+ Treg cells maintain immune homeostasis in healthy people. In tumours, FOXP3+ Treg cells have strong immunosuppressive function that may promote immune escape of cancer cells and tumour development. Tumour-derived factors can also modulate the induction, migration, and immunosuppressive function of FOXP3+ Treg cells. In the context of cancer angiogenesis, VEGF could promote FOXP3+ regulatory T cell migration and its immunosuppressive function. In human cancers, antiangiogenic therapy blocking VEGFR is associated with a reduction of tumour-infiltrating FOXP3+ regulatory T cells. The detailed mechanisms linking VEGFR blockade to this reduction are still unclear. In colorectal cancer tumour microenvironments, tissue-resident FOXP3+ Treg subsets include FOXP3(lo) non-suppressive cells that lack CD45RA, show FOXP3 instability, and secrete inflammatory cytokines such as IFN-γ. Fusobacterium nucleatum and other intestinal bacteria induce tumour production of IL-12, TGF-β, and TNF-α and thereby facilitate expansion of pro-inflammatory FOXP3(lo) non-Treg cells. Expansion of these FOXP3(lo) non-Treg cells enhances anti-tumour immunity and inhibits tumour formation. Colorectal cancer patients with abundant FOXP3(lo) infiltration are predicted to have better survival.

The inflammatory response could help our body to remove the necrotic tissue and tumours, so it was once considered as a beneficial anti-tumour immune response. However, subsequent studies have demonstrated that the inflammatory process in TME can also lead to the emergence of tumour invasive metastasis, angiogenesis and other tumour-promoting features ( 84 ). FOXP3+ Treg cells may play an important role in regulating the balance of tumour inflammation. Traditionally, Treg cells are believed to be the main anti-inflammatory cells in humans, which suppress the function of immune cells and reduce the inflammatory response, resulting in a poorer prognosis in cancer patients. However, more recent studies have revealed the existence of different tissue resident FOXP3+ Treg cell subsets in CRC, in contrast to the classical Treg cell immunosuppressive function, can also exhibit a pro-inflammatory response profile and thus influence the development and progression of CRC ( 85 ). Saito and colleagues have grouped CRCs into two types, based on the proportion of FOXP3(lo) non-suppressive T cells ( 85 ). FOXP3(lo) Treg cells are distinguished from FOXP3 (+) T cells in the absence of the naïve T cell marker CD45RA, FOXP3 instability, and enhanced secretion of inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IFN-γ) by the FOXP3(lo) Treg subset ( 85 ). CRCs patients with abundant FOXP3(lo) Treg cell infiltration are predicted to have better survival. Mechanistically, Fusobacterium nucleatum, and possibly other intestinal bacteria mediate tumor tissues’ production of inflammatory cytokines (e.g., IL-12, TGF-β, and TNF-α) ( 85 – 90 ), thereby affecting the heterogeneity of tumour-infiltrating Treg cells in CRCs and facilitating the expansion of pro-inflammatory FOXP3(lo) non-Treg cells that, in turn, enhances anti-tumour immunity and inhibits tumour formation ( 85 ).

Treg cells, also known as suppressor T cells, are a subpopulation of T cells that modulate the immune system ( 7 ). The lineage determining transcription factor, FOXP3 forms a large molecular complex with multiple transcription factors and enzymatic subunits to dynamically regulate the development and function of regulatory T cells ( 8 – 14 ). FOXP3+ Treg cells play essential roles in maintaining immune homeostasis in healthy people ( 15 ).
