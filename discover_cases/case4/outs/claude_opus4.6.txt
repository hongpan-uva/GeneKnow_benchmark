FOXP3 (Forkhead box P3) is a transcription factor that serves as the master regulator of regulatory T cell (Treg) development and function. FOXP3 is both necessary and sufficient for the programming of Treg cell lineage commitment in the thymus, as demonstrated by the finding that ectopic expression of FOXP3 in conventional CD4+ T cells converts them into cells with suppressive capacity (Hori, S., Nomura, T., & Sakaguchi, S., 2003). Loss-of-function mutations in *FOXP3* result in the fatal lymphoproliferative autoimmune disorder known as IPEX (immune dysregulation, polyendocrinopathy, enteropathy, X-linked) syndrome in humans, underscoring its non-redundant role in immune tolerance (Bennett, C. L., et al., 2001). In mice, the *scurfy* mutation in *Foxp3* produces a similar multi-organ autoimmune phenotype due to the absence of functional Tregs (Brunkow, M. E., et al., 2001). Mechanistically, FOXP3 operates in part by repressing the transcription of cytokine genes such as *IL2* and *IL4* while activating expression of Treg-associated molecules including *CTLA-4*, *GITR*, and *CD25* (IL-2Rα) (Zheng, Y., & Rudensky, A. Y., 2007). Continuous FOXP3 expression is required to maintain the Treg suppressive program, as its ablation in mature Tregs causes them to acquire effector T cell functions (Williams, L. M., & Rudensky, A. Y., 2007).
