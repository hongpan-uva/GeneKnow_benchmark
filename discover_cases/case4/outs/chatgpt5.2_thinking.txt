FOXP3 is selectively expressed in CD4⁺CD25⁺ regulatory T cells (Tregs) and its expression correlates with suppressive activity in these cells. ([PubMed][1]) In mice, loss of Foxp3 (including Foxp3-null and scurfy mutation contexts examined) causes a lethal lymphoproliferative autoimmune syndrome that the authors attribute to a deficiency of CD4⁺CD25⁺ Tregs rather than an intrinsic defect of conventional CD4⁺CD25⁻ T cells. ([PubMed][1]) Forced/ectopic expression of Foxp3 in naïve peripheral CD4⁺ T cells (via retroviral transduction) is sufficient to induce a regulatory T-cell–like phenotype, including acquisition of suppressive function and characteristic changes in activation/cytokine features described by the authors. ([PubMed][2]) In humans, mutations in FOXP3 were reported as the genetic cause of the immune dysregulation, polyendocrinopathy, enteropathy, X-linked (IPEX) syndrome in affected patients studied, directly linking FOXP3 disruption to this immunodysregulatory disease. ([PubMed][3])
