FOXP3, a forkhead/winged-helix transcription factor, is the lineage-defining regulator essential for the development, maintenance, and suppressive function of regulatory T cells (Tregs). Mutations in the **Foxp3** gene result in the "Scurfy" phenotype in mice and IPEX syndrome in humans, both characterized by fatal systemic autoimmunity due to a total deficiency of functional Tregs (Wildin et al., 2001; Brunkow et al., 2001). FOXP3 is required for the commitment of thymic precursors to the Treg lineage and the acquisition of their characteristic suppressive phenotype (Fontenot et al., 2003; Hori et al., 2003). Mechanistically, FOXP3 acts as both a transcriptional activator and repressor; it directly upregulates the expression of Treg-associated markers such as **CD25**, **CTLA-4**, and **GITR**, while actively repressing the production of effector cytokines, including **IL-2** and **IFN-$\gamma$** (Wu et al., 2006; Williams & Rudensky, 2007). This transcriptional program is facilitated by the physical interaction of FOXP3 with **NFAT**, which reconfigures the T-cell activation program toward immune suppression (Wu et al., 2006). Furthermore, continuous FOXP3 expression is mandatory in mature Tregs to prevent their dedifferentiation into pro-inflammatory effector-like cells (Williams & Rudensky, 2007).
