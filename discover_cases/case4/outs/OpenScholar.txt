## FOXP3 — regulatory T cells (Tregs)

**Query:** What functional relationships between FOXP3 and regulatory T cells (Tregs) have been directly reported in peer-reviewed research articles?

FOXP3 is a crucial transcription factor for the development and function of regulatory T cells (Tregs) [0]. Tregs are instrumental in maintaining immune homeostasis and preventing autoimmunity [0]. However, under pro-inflammatory conditions, FOXP3 expression in Tregs can become unstable, leading to loss of suppressive function and conversion into pathogenic T effector cells [0]. The success of adoptive cell therapy with chimeric antigen receptor (CAR) Tregs is highly dependent on the stability of FOXP3 expression [0]. FOXP3+ Tregs have been found to play a role in anti-inflammatory responses, with glucocorticoids (GC) inducing miR-342–3p in Tregs, which targets the mTORC2 component Rictor, regulating metabolic programming in Tregs and controlling their regulatory functions [1]. In contrast, breast cancer stem cells (CSCs) have been found to convert anti-tumor CD4+ T cells to pro-tumor Tregs through the transfer of FOXP3 via exosomes [2]. Additionally, FOXP3+ Tregs have been shown to secrete IL-17 and constitutively express the TH17 lineage-specific transcription factor RORγt, suggesting a pro-inflammatory function [4]. The epigenetic regulation of FOXP3, including DNA methylation, histone modifications, and post-translational modifications, governs the development and optimal suppressive function of Tregs [5]. Furthermore, Tregs can be converted from peripheral CD4+CD25− naive T cells through costimulation with T cell receptors and transforming growth factor β (TGF-β) [6]. Lastly, direct targeting of FOXP3 in Tregs with antisense oligonucleotides (ASOs) has been shown to modulate the activity of Tregs and augment antitumor immunity [7]. Furthermore, FOXP3 reprograms T cell metabolism by suppressing Myc and glycolysis, enhancing oxidative phosphorylation, and increasing nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide oxidation, allowing Tregs to function in low glucose, lactate-rich environments [8]. A systematic review and meta-analysis found that higher tumor-infiltrating FOXP3+ Tregs are associated with poor prognosis in breast cancer, particularly in c-erbB-2 positive status, lymph node positive status, and estrogen receptor (ER) negative status [9]. In summary, FOXP3 is a critical transcription factor for Tregs that plays a complex role in regulating immune responses [0][1][2][4][5][6][7][8]. Understanding the functional relationships between FOXP3 and Tregs can provide insights into the regulation of immune homeostasis and autoimmunity [5].
