OLIG2 is a basic helix–loop–helix transcription factor whose expression marks ventral spinal cord progenitors that generate the oligodendrocyte lineage, and genetic loss-of-function studies show it is required for oligodendrocyte formation in vivo: in mouse spinal cord, Olig2 disruption eliminates oligodendrocyte specification/differentiation from these progenitors (Lu et al., 2002; Takebayashi et al., 2002), and Olig1/Olig2 double-mutant mice similarly show abolished oligodendrocyte differentiation with lineage-tracing evidence that the affected pMN progenitors instead produce other neural cell types (Zhou & Anderson, 2002). In zebrafish, olig2 function is likewise required for oligodendrocyte development (Park et al., 2002). Gain-of-function experiments demonstrate that OLIG2 can actively drive oligodendrocyte differentiation in defined contexts: in the chick spinal cord, coexpression of Olig2 with Nkx2.2 promotes ectopic and precocious oligodendrocyte differentiation (Zhou et al., 2001), and in mouse neural stem cell differentiation assays, induced Olig2 expression is sufficient to bias fate toward oligodendrocyte specification (Du et al., 2006). In vivo inducible overexpression of Olig2 in Sox10+ oligodendroglial cells increases differentiated oligodendrocytes, accelerates developmental myelination, and enhances remyelination after focal demyelination (Wegener et al., 2015).
