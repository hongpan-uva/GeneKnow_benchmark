FOXR2 (Forkhead Box R2) has two distinct, well-documented relationships with neuroblastoma. First, FOXR2 activation is the defining molecular feature of CNS neuroblastoma, FOXR2-activated (NB-FOXR2), a pediatric brain tumor entity recognized in the 2021 WHO Classification of Central Nervous System Tumors; these supratentorial tumors arise primarily in the cerebral hemispheres of young children (mean age ~6 years), are driven by structural rearrangements and fusions that lead to FOXR2 overexpression and subsequent MYC (but not MYCN) upregulation, and carry a relatively favorable prognosis compared to other embryonal tumors when treated with surgery, craniospinal irradiation, and chemotherapy (Jessa et al., 2025; Korshunov et al., 2021). Second, in extracranial (sympathoadrenal) neuroblastoma, approximately 9% of tumors express FOXR2 despite lacking MYCN amplification; FOXR2 physically binds and stabilizes MYCN protein at the post-translational level, representing an alternative mechanism to MYCN amplification for elevating MYCN protein levels, and FOXR2 expression in this context is an independent prognostic factor associated with unfavorable outcome, with 10-year overall survival rates of 53–59% (Schmitt-Hoffner et al., 2021). Additionally, a human embryonic stem cell–based model has identified medial ganglionic eminence progenitors as the likely cell of origin of CNS NB-FOXR2, with evidence that FOXR2 activates the MEK/ERK signaling pathway through suppression of DIRAS3 (Royston et al., 2024).
