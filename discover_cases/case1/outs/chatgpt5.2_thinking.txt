SOX9 is a transcription factor with repeatedly demonstrated roles in astrocyte lineage biology across development, adult CNS, and cellular reprogramming. In developing mouse spinal cord, conditional ablation of Sox9 in neural stem cells causes defects in glial development, including impaired astrocyte specification, supporting an essential developmental requirement for Sox9 in generating astrocytes. ([PubMed][1]) During the initiation of gliogenesis, Sox9 regulates induction of the gliogenic factor NFIA, and Sox9 and NFIA form a complex that coregulates downstream genes with reported roles in astroglial precursor physiology (including migration- and metabolism-related targets) during astrogliogenesis. ([PubMed][2]) In neural stem/progenitor contexts, Notch-driven astrogliogenesis depends on Sox9: knockdown of Notch1-induced Sox9 expression reverses Notch1-induced astroglial differentiation and related lineage-output changes. ([PubMed][3]) In the adult mouse and human brain outside neurogenic regions, SOX9 is reported as an astrocyte-enriched nuclear marker (with notable expression also in ependymal cells and neural progenitors in neurogenic zones) and is upregulated in reactive astrocytes across multiple injury/disease models examined in that study. ([PubMed][4]) Experimentally, forced expression of NFIA/NFIB/SOX9 is sufficient to convert mouse fibroblasts into induced astrocytes with molecular and functional properties comparable to native astrocytes. ([科学直接][5]) Inducible NFIA plus SOX9 expression in human pluripotent stem cells accelerates generation of astrocytes whose astrocytic identity was validated by molecular and functional assays (including after transplantation). ([Addgene][6])
