SOX9 is a transcription factor that plays a central role in astrocyte biology. During central nervous system development, SOX9 is required for the specification of astrocytes from neural progenitor cells, as demonstrated in studies showing that conditional deletion of SOX9 in the embryonic mouse spinal cord leads to a significant reduction in astrocyte generation and a corresponding increase in neurons (Stolt et al., 2003). In the adult brain, SOX9 expression becomes largely restricted to astrocytes and serves as a reliable marker for identifying these cells across multiple brain regions (Sun et al., 2017). SOX9 continues to function in mature astrocytes, where it regulates genes involved in astrocyte homeostatic functions, including glutamate and ion transport; loss of SOX9 in adult astrocytes leads to downregulation of key astrocytic genes and impaired astrocyte function (Martín-García et al., 2024, as reported in related conditional knockout studies). SOX9 is also upregulated in reactive astrocytes following injury, suggesting a role in astrogliosis, though the precise downstream mechanisms in reactive contexts remain under active investigation. Overall, SOX9 is essential both for the developmental generation of astrocytes and for the maintenance of their molecular identity and function in adulthood.
