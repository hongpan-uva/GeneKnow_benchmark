## SOX9 — astrocytes or astrocytic differentiation

**Query:** What functional relationships between SOX9 and astrocytes or astrocytic differentiation have been directly reported in peer-reviewed research articles?

SOX9 is a transcriptional regulator of mature astrocyte function in the mouse olfactory bulb (OB) [1], and its deletion in OB astrocytes impairs neuronal sensory processing [1]. In the mouse brain, SOX9 is an astrocyte-specific nuclear marker outside of the neurogenic regions [2]. Astrocytic Sox9 is essential for physiological sensory processing in the adult mouse OB [1]. Astrocytes regulate neuronal activity, and Sox9 deletion results in impaired neuronal sensory processing in the mouse adult olfactory bulb [1]. Furthermore, the expression of Sox9 is increased in chronic-phase astrocytes (CAs) compared to scar-forming astrocytes (SAs) in the sub-acute phase [3], and Sox9 is a crucial transcriptional regulator of glial cell differentiation [5]. Astrocytes are emerging as potential pharmacological targets for antipsychotics such as clozapine, which may modulate their function by regulating glutamate clearance, redox balance, and synaptic remodeling [4]. Astrocytes are required for physiological sensory processing and identify astrocytic Sox9 as an essential transcriptional regulator of mature astrocyte function in the mouse OB [1]. Astrocytes contribute to forming an aberrant substrate during early neurodevelopment, potentially predisposing individuals to later neurodegeneration [4]. Moreover, the Hedgehog (Hh) signaling pathway induces expression of USP18 through Gli2-mediated transcriptional activation after SCI, and administration of the Hh pathway activator SAG significantly increases reactive astrogliosis [7]. Astrocytes are emerging key players in neurological disorders, but their role in disease etiology is poorly understood due to inaccessibility of primary human astrocytes [8]. Astrocytes regulate neuronal activity and are required for physiological sensory processing [1].
