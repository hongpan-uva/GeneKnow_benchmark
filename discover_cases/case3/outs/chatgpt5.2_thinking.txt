PAX5 (also called BSAP) is functionally required for establishing and sustaining B-cell identity. In mice, germline disruption of **Pax5** causes a complete block of B-cell differentiation at an early stage of B lymphopoiesis, demonstrating that Pax5 is necessary for progression beyond early B-cell progenitors (Urbánek et al., 1994). Pax5 is also required for **commitment** to the B-lymphoid lineage: Pax5-deficient lymphoid progenitors fail to complete B-lineage commitment, supporting a direct role for Pax5 in initiating the B-cell developmental program (Nutt et al., 1999). Using conditional inactivation, loss of Pax5 in **committed pro-B cells** leads to reversion from a committed B-cell state to hematopoietic progenitors, showing that continued Pax5 expression is required to **maintain** early B-cell lineage commitment (Mikkola et al., 2002). Conditional Pax5 deletion in later B cells causes downregulation of B-cell surface antigens and B-cell gene expression with activation of non–B-lineage genes, supporting an essential role for Pax5 in maintaining B-cell identity and function during late B lymphopoiesis (Horcher et al., 2001). Mechanistically, Pax5 directly activates the **BLNK** gene to control pre-BCR signaling, linking Pax5 activity to signaling pathways needed for early B-cell development (Schebesta et al., 2002), and Pax5-mediated repression of lineage-inappropriate genes is continuously required and is reversed in plasma cells (Delogu et al., 2006).
