PAX5 is the master regulator of B-cell identity, functioning as a dual-purpose transcription factor that simultaneously activates B-cell-specific programs and represses alternative hematopoietic fates. It is indispensable for B-cell commitment; the loss of PAX5 results in a complete developmental block at the early pro-B cell stage (Urbanek et al., 1994). PAX5 directly promotes B-cell signaling and development by activating essential genes such as **Cd19** and **Blnk**, while actively silencing lineage-inappropriate genes like **Csf1r** and **Notch1** (Nutt et al., 1999). Furthermore, PAX5 is critical for immunoglobulin heavy-chain ($Igh$) gene rearrangement, as it facilitates the physical contraction of the $Igh$ locus to enable distal $V_H$-to-$DJ_H$ recombination (Fuxa et al., 2004). Beyond initial differentiation, the functional presence of PAX5 is required to maintain lineage fidelity; its conditional deletion in mature B cells causes them to dedifferentiate into multipotent progenitor-like cells that can subsequently adopt T-cell or myeloid identities (Cobaleda et al., 2007). Essentially, PAX5 acts as both the "architect" and the "security guard" of the B-cell state.
