PAX5 is a paired box transcription factor that serves as a master regulator of B cell identity and commitment throughout B lymphopoiesis. PAX5 is essential for commitment of lymphoid progenitors to the B cell lineage, as demonstrated by the finding that PAX5-deficient progenitors arrest at the pro-B cell stage and retain multilineage differentiation potential (Nutt et al., 1999). PAX5 activates transcription of B cell–specific genes, including *CD19*, *BLNK*, and *MB-1* (encoding Igα), while simultaneously repressing lineage-inappropriate genes such as *Notch1* and *M-CSF receptor* (*Fms*), thereby enforcing B cell identity (Nutt et al., 1999; Revilla-i-Domingo et al., 2012). Conditional deletion of PAX5 in mature B cells causes them to dedifferentiate into uncommitted progenitors capable of generating T cells and other lineages, establishing that PAX5 is required not only for initial commitment but also for ongoing maintenance of B cell identity (Cobaleda et al., 2007). Furthermore, PAX5 is recurrently mutated or rearranged in B cell precursor acute lymphoblastic leukemia, implicating its haploinsufficiency in leukemogenesis (Mullighan et al., 2007).
