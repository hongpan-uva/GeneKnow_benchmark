PU.1, encoded by the **Spi1** gene, is the master transcription factor governing the specification, differentiation, and functional maintenance of the macrophage lineage. During early hematopoiesis, high-level expression of PU.1 is required to commit multipotent progenitors to the myeloid fate; specifically, high PU.1 concentrations drive macrophage differentiation, whereas lower levels favor B-cell or neutrophil development (DeKoter & Singh, 2000). PU.1 directly transactivates the promoter of the **Colony-Stimulating Factor 1 Receptor** (*Csf1r*), an essential regulator of macrophage survival and proliferation (Zhang et al., 1994). In mature macrophages, PU.1 functions as a **pioneer transcription factor**, binding to distal enhancers to establish an open chromatin landscape that allows for the recruitment of signal-dependent factors like $NF-\kappa B$ (Heinz et al., 2010). It is also required for the proper execution of the innate immune response, as PU.1 facilitates the recruitment of transcriptional machinery to pro-inflammatory gene loci following stimulation (Ghisletti et al., 2010). Consequently, the absolute loss of PU.1 results in a complete failure of macrophage development in vivo (Scott et al., 1994).
