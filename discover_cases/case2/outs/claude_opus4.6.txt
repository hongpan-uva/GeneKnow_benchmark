PU.1 (encoded by the *SPI1* gene) is a member of the ETS family of transcription factors that serves as a master regulator of myeloid cell development, and it is both necessary and sufficient for macrophage differentiation. Studies using PU.1-knockout mice demonstrated that these animals lack macrophages entirely, establishing that PU.1 is essential for macrophage generation from hematopoietic progenitors (McKercher et al., 1996). PU.1 exerts its pro-macrophage function in part by directly activating the promoters of macrophage-specific genes, including the CSF1 receptor (*Csf1r*), which is critical for macrophage survival and proliferation (Aikawa et al., 2010). The concentration of PU.1 within progenitor cells is itself instructive: high levels of PU.1 favor macrophage fate, whereas low levels promote B-lymphocyte development, indicating a dose-dependent lineage commitment mechanism (DeKoter & Singh, 2000). Beyond development, PU.1 continues to function in mature macrophages by cooperating with other transcription factors such as C/EBPα to establish the enhancer landscape that governs macrophage-specific gene expression programs, including those activated during inflammatory responses (Ghisletti et al., 2010). PU.1 also directly regulates genes involved in macrophage effector functions such as phagocytosis and cytokine production (Celada et al., 1996).
