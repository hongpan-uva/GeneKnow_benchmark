PU.1 (encoded by *SPI1*) is a transcription factor with experimentally demonstrated roles in establishing and regulating macrophage lineage programs. In gene-targeted mice, disruption of *PU.1* causes a consistent defect in generating progenitors for monocytes (a macrophage lineage precursor), alongside other hematopoietic lineages, indicating PU.1 is required for normal development of these progenitor compartments. ([PubMed][1]) Mechanistically, PU.1 directly binds a PU.1 site in the human *CSF1R* (M-CSF receptor) promoter; mutating that site reduces promoter activity in macrophage cell lines, and ectopic PU.1 can transactivate the promoter in non-macrophage cells, supporting a direct role for PU.1 in driving *CSF1R* transcriptional output. ([PubMed][2]) At the genome-regulatory level in tissue macrophages, PU.1 motifs are prevalent in macrophage enhancer regions, and the macrophage enhancer landscape is described as being shaped by local microenvironmental cues. ([PubMed][3]) Consistently, comparative enhancer/transcriptome analyses across resident macrophage populations show that tissue environments promote divergent gene-expression programs by inducing secondary transcription factors that collaborate with PU.1 to establish tissue-specific enhancers. ([PubMed][4]) Finally, enforced expression studies demonstrate functional sufficiency in reprogramming contexts: PU.1 together with C/EBP factors can convert fibroblasts into macrophage-like cells exhibiting macrophage phenotypic features. ([美国国家科学院院刊][5])
