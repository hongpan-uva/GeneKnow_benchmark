## PU.1 — macrophages

**Query:** What functional relationships between PU.1 (SPI1) and macrophages have been directly reported in peer-reviewed research articles?

PU.1 (Spi1) plays a crucial role in regulating macrophage development, function, and inflammatory response [0][2][4]. It is a transcription factor that is essential for myeloid cell development and is involved in the regulation of various macrophage-specific genes [2][6]. In addition to its role in regulating macrophage development and function, PU.1 is also involved in regulating the microglial/macrophage inflammatory response via the PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling pathway [1], and its expression levels can modulate the microglial/macrophage transcriptome [1]. Moreover, PU.1 can regulate the expression of pro-inflammatory cytokines and the phagocytic uptake of macrophages [0][4][7]. PU.1 has also been shown to interact with other transcription factors, such as Ets-2 and Elf-1, to regulate the transcription of macrophage-specific genes [6]. Furthermore, PU.1 can bind to the promoters of genes such as Ccl22 and Ccl7 to regulate their expression in dendritic cells and macrophages [8][7], and it can also bind to the promoter of vascular endothelial growth factor A and facilitate angiogenesis [9]. In addition, PU.1 can regulate macrophage polarization, and PU.1+CD68+ macrophages have been identified as a biomarker for gastric cancer metastasis [9]. The level of SPI1+CD68+ TAMs infiltration has been shown to be closely related to the efficacy of immunotherapy, especially when combined with antiangiogenic therapy [9]. In summary, PU.1 plays a critical role in regulating macrophage development, function, and inflammatory response, and its dysregulation has been implicated in various diseases, including Alzheimer's disease [0], intracerebral hemorrhage [1], and cancer [7][9]. Further research is needed to fully understand the functional relationships between PU.1 and macrophages and to explore its potential as a therapeutic target.
