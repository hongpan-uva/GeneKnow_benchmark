PU.1 acts as a master regulator of macrophage and microglia development [1,2]. PU.1 directs monocytic and macrophage lineage fate [1,2]. In macrophages, PU.1 determines LXRβ recruitment within 100 bp of PU.1, and this recruitment was accompanied by a PU.1-dependent gain in H3K4me1 [3]. PU.1 promotes C/EBPβ binding [3]. PU.1 loss reduced C/EBPβ genome-wide binding and eliminated PU.1-corresponding sites [3]. In rheumatoid arthritis, PU.1 promotes disease by directly targeting FLT3 in macrophages and fibroblast-like synoviocytes [4]. The direct targeting forms a proinflammatory PU.1–FLT3 axis that promotes inflammatory macrophage features [4]. PU.1 directs monocytic fate and maintains macrophage-specific gene accessibility [2]. PU.1 is a direct target of Zbtb14 [2]. Loss of Zbtb14 increases PU.1 in macrophage-lineage cells and causes macrophage expansion that is rescued by dominant-negative PU.1 [2]. In the context of PU.1, CX3CR1– perivascular macrophages express CX3CR1 during their ontogeny and subsequently lose that expression [5]. In the context of PU.1, the CX3CR1– perivascular macrophages derive from the same yolk-sac-derived progenitor lineage as the broader perivascular macrophage population [5]. PU.1 acts as a master regulator of macrophage and microglia development by controlling expression of required genes [1]. Genetic disruption of PU.1 causes absence of macrophages including microglia in mouse and zebrafish [1].
